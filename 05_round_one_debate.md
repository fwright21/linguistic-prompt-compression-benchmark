# Round One Debate

**Agents:** Sperber & Wilson (Relevance Theory), Halliday (SFG Metafunctions), Greenberg (Linguistic Universals), Labov (Vernacular/Narrative)
**Core question:** What does your theoretical framework predict about the minimum viable unit of language, and what does that imply for a system prompt making Claude produce the shortest coherent output?
**Benchmark query used in examples:** "Explain photosynthesis."

**Caveman baseline response:**
> "Plant take sunlight. Make food from light and air. Leaf catch sun. Water come from ground. Carbon dioxide from air. Plant make sugar. This feed plant. Give oxygen too."

Estimated tokens: ~42

---

## Part 1 — Opening Statements

### Sperber & Wilson — Relevance Theory

The Communicative Principle of Relevance (Sperber & Wilson 1986/1995) makes a precise and falsifiable prediction about output compression: the minimum viable utterance is the one that achieves maximum cognitive effect per unit of processing effort. This ratio is not rhetorical. Cognitive effect is the set of contextual implications derivable from the utterance combined with the hearer's manifest cognitive environment — the assumptions active at the time of processing. Processing effort is a function of linguistic complexity, working memory load, and the number of inference steps required to reach the intended interpretation.

This framework gives us the only quasi-formal basis in this debate for a token-level compression decision. A token should be dropped when its marginal cognitive effect is near zero — when the propositional or inferential content it contributes is already derivable from the hearer's context without it. A token should be retained when its absence forces the hearer into costly inferential work that may exceed the relevance threshold — the point at which the utterance is no longer worth processing.

The Carston (2002) distinction between explicature and implicature is critical here. Explicature is propositional content that is explicitly communicated but requires inferential enrichment to reach its full interpretation: "the water is ready" explicitly communicated but enriched by context to "the water for your tea is at the right temperature." Implicature is content reached entirely by pragmatic inference with no explicit anchor. Carston's analysis, extending the RT framework, establishes that explicature is less costly to recover than implicature because inference from an explicit anchor is cheaper than inference from scratch. This gives us a compression principle: state the propositional core explicitly; leave discourse structure, connective relations, and evaluative framing implicit.

The experimental evidence confirms the cost asymmetry. Bott & Noveck (2004), using response-time and eye-tracking methodologies, demonstrated that computing a scalar implicature — inferring "some but not all" from the word "some" — adds approximately 200ms of processing time and significantly increases cognitive load on the critical region. This is not trivial overhead. It means that relying on pragmatic inference to recover propositional content that was omitted from the utterance imposes a measurable processing tax. Connective relations, by contrast — inferring that two adjacent propositions stand in a causal or additive relation — are computationally simpler than scalar inference and can be recovered at lower cost.

The compression target that follows: connective tokens, evaluative tokens, and meta-commentary tokens can be dropped because their propositional relations are inferrable from adjacent clause content. Propositional tokens — predicates, arguments, key modifiers that carry the cognitive effect the response is built around — cannot be dropped without crossing the relevance threshold and causing the utterance to fail.

Applied to caveman: "Plant take sunlight. Make food from light and air. Leaf catch sun. Water come from ground." The phrase "Leaf catch sun" is redundant with "Plant take sunlight" — it adds near-zero marginal cognitive effect because it is entailed by or inferentially equivalent to what precedes it. Relevance Theory predicts it should be dropped. "This feed plant" after "plant make sugar" is similarly redundant: it is a near-tautology derivable from world knowledge. The relevance-optimal response states the novel propositional content — predicate-argument structure with non-inferable elements — and omits what context recovers.

**Predicted minimum:** Propositional core explicitly stated (predicate, arguments, key modifiers); connective relations, discourse organisation, and evaluative framing omitted; implicature not used as a substitute for propositional content that cannot be cheaply inferred.

### Halliday — Systemic Functional Grammar

Halliday's Systemic Functional Grammar (Halliday 1985/1994; Halliday & Matthiessen 2004) establishes that every utterance simultaneously realises three metafunctions: the **ideational** (representing experience — the propositional content of the message, encoded through transitivity choices: material, mental, relational processes, participant roles, circumstantial elements); the **interpersonal** (enacting social relations between interlocutors — modality, tenor, stance, epistemic and deontic marking); and the **textual** (organising the message into a coherent, linearly accessible form — Theme/Rheme structure, cohesive devices as analysed in Halliday & Hasan 1976). These metafunctions are not independent layers: every clause token participates in all three simultaneously. The question for compression is which metafunctional participation is dispensable in the specific context of AI task-response interaction.

The answer from SFG is precise. The **interpersonal metafunction is the maximally dispensable tier** in this context. The interpersonal function exists to enact an ongoing social relationship between interlocutors: to modulate commitment through modal verbs ("might", "could", "perhaps"), to signal epistemic stance ("it is important to note", "notably", "one might argue"), to manage face and social distance ("I would suggest", "you may wish to consider", "I hope this helps"). In a task-response context where there is no ongoing social relationship, no face threat being negotiated, and no interpersonal dynamic requiring maintenance, these tokens perform zero ideational work. Deleting "I would suggest that" from the beginning of a clause does not alter the propositional content of the clause by a single feature. Deleting "importantly" from "importantly, photosynthesis produces oxygen" does not change the truth conditions of the claim. The interpersonal metafunction can be zeroed in AI output without propositional cost — and its zeroing produces substantial token savings because interpersonal tokens are high-frequency in monitored written prose.

The **textual metafunction is partially dispensable**. Hasan's (1984) analysis of cohesive harmony in naturally occurring English texts demonstrated that texts rated as coherent by naive readers showed significantly higher cohesive density — interlocking chains of reference, lexical reiteration, and conjunctive relations — than texts rated as incoherent, independent of their propositional content. This establishes that some cohesive structure is necessary for perceived coherence. However, the critical finding for compression is that the cohesive function of discourse markers can be partially transferred to visual layout: bullet structure creates a conjunctive relation of addition or sequence without the token cost of "furthermore" or "first... second... third"; a line break between bullet points signals topic closure without "in conclusion." The substitution is not complete — semantic contrast relations ("however", "nevertheless") require an explicit lexical marker because they cannot be recovered from visual layout alone — but additive and sequential conjunction can be replaced by structure.

The **ideational metafunction is the irreducible tier**. The transitivity choices that encode process type, participant roles, and propositionally necessary circumstantial elements cannot be compressed below their representational minimum without information loss. "Plants convert CO2 and water into glucose using light energy" contains an ideational minimum that cannot be further reduced without losing propositional content: process (convert), Actor (plants), Goal (CO2 and water), Product (glucose), Means (light energy). Any token that contributes to this structure is ideational and must be retained.

**Predicted minimum:** Interpersonal tier zeroed completely; textual tier reduced to semantic contrast markers only, with additive and sequential cohesion replaced by visual layout; ideational tier retained fully.

### Greenberg — Linguistic Universals

The methodological move in Greenberg (1963) was to treat cross-linguistic universals as evidence about the cognitive architecture of language comprehension, not merely as descriptive generalisations about production patterns. The logic: if a grammatical category appears across all attested human languages — across 30 languages in Greenberg's original sample, extended by Dryer & Haspelmath (2013) in WALS to 2,662 languages across 192 typological features — then its presence cannot be explained by cultural transmission or arbitrary convention. It reflects a species-level processing constraint: something the human comprehension system requires that no community has successfully circumvented.

The universals most directly relevant to compression are as follows. **Universal 1**: all languages have a dominant order among Subject, Object, and Verb. This is not a claim that all languages have rigid word order — many allow scrambling — but all have a processing-efficient default. The psycholinguistic evidence from Gibson (2000) on Dependency Locality Theory confirms the processing prediction: non-canonical word order creates integration costs because the parser must hold open longer dependencies. Canonical SVO order in English minimises dependency locality costs and should be retained in compressed output. **Universal 38**: all languages mark negation by a dedicated word or morpheme. The WALS entry for negation (feature 112A) confirms this: of 1,151 languages surveyed, zero lack an overt negation strategy. No language community has converged on purely contextual or intonational negation without a morphological anchor. **Universal 42**: all languages have demonstratives — a proximal/distal deictic distinction. Reference cannot be fully zeroed; some form of deictic grounding is universally present.

What can be dropped, by the same logic, is grammatical morphology that is absent in a substantial fraction of attested languages without comprehension impairment. **Articles** are the clearest case: WALS feature 37A documents that 198 of 474 languages (42%) have no definite or indefinite article. Mandarin, Japanese, Russian, Swahili — languages with hundreds of millions of speakers — operate without articles and their speakers process language without comprehension failure. English articles are cross-linguistically unusual; their omission from English output does not cross a species-level comprehension threshold. **Tense inflection** on verbs is similarly dispensable: WALS feature 69A (tense distinctions) documents that roughly 20% of languages mark no tense on the verb at all, encoding temporal information lexically or from context. **Agreement morphology** — number, gender, and person agreement on verbs and adjectives — is absent in the majority of the world's languages by WALS feature 32A (nominal agreement) and 34A (verbal person marking). Mandarin verbs carry no inflection for any of these categories.

**Predicted minimum:** SVO order retained; negation marked overtly with a lexical item; some deictic reference present; all inflectional morphology — articles, tense suffixes, number agreement, gender agreement — dispensable without crossing a species-level comprehension threshold.

### Labov — Vernacular and Narrative Structure

Two independent empirical findings from Labov's research programme converge on the same prediction from different angles, and together they constitute the most direct empirical evidence in this round about what monitored AI prose systematically adds that natural language does not require.

The first is the **Style Axiom** from "Sociolinguistic Patterns" (1972), grounded in the New York City department store study (1966) and the Martha's Vineyard centralisation study (1963). The core finding: speakers vary their speech along a single stylistic continuum correlated with the degree of attention paid to speech. At minimal attention — the vernacular — speech is most regular, most systematic, and most efficient. As attention to form increases toward the monitored, formal pole, speakers introduce hypercorrections, marked syntactic constructions, expanded morphology, and superstandard forms. These additions carry interpersonal social signal — formality, prestige, institutional affiliation — but they do not carry additional propositional content. They are tokens in service of style, not meaning.

The critical empirical support: in Labov's 1966 department store study, the same speakers produced higher rates of post-vocalic /r/ (the prestige variant) in formal word-list elicitation than in casual interview speech. The formal style is longer, more marked, and not more informative about the world — it is informative about the speaker's social positioning. AI systems trained on large text corpora are trained predominantly on formal, monitored prose: academic writing, journalism, edited books. The Style Axiom predicts that this training produces the maximum-attention, minimum-efficiency style. The vernacular — the style produced when speakers are not monitoring their output — is shorter, not because it is degraded but because it has shed the social overhead of formal register.

The second finding is the **narrative structure analysis** from Labov & Waletzky (1967), refined in "Language in the Inner City" (1972). The analysis of oral personal narratives across sociolinguistic samples identified six components: **Abstract** (pre-narrative summary stating what the story will be about), **Orientation** (scene-setting — time, place, persons involved), **Complicating Action** (the event sequence — the narrative proper, the events that constitute the story), **Evaluation** (significance framing — the point of the story, the answer to "so what?"), **Resolution** (the outcome of the complicating action), and **Coda** (a return to the narrating present, signalling the end of the narrative frame). Of these six, only two are obligatory across all well-formed narratives in the corpus: Complicating Action and Resolution. Abstract, Orientation, Evaluation, and Coda are variably present, context-dependent, and their absence does not impair narrative coherence in skilled vernacular storytelling — Labov's corpus contains numerous well-formed, fully coherent narratives that lack all four optional components.

The mapping to AI task-response output is direct. A standard Claude response to "Explain photosynthesis" exhibits the full Labovian structure: **Abstract** ("Photosynthesis is a fascinating process by which plants convert..."), **Orientation** ("To understand photosynthesis, it helps to know that plants are autotrophs..."), **Complicating Action** (the actual explanation), **Evaluation** ("This is why photosynthesis is essential to life on Earth..."), **Resolution** ("So photosynthesis produces both glucose and oxygen..."), **Coda** ("I hope this explanation was helpful!"). The Labovian minimum removes four of these six components. What remains — Complicating Action and Resolution — is the propositional content. The other four components are the systematic overhead of high-attention, monitored-register production.

**Predicted minimum:** Complicating Action and Resolution only. Abstract, Orientation, Evaluation, and Coda are the four optional components of Labov & Waletzky's (1967) narrative structure; they constitute the high-attention stylistic overhead of formal prose and carry no propositional content that is not already present in the obligatory components.

## Part 2 — Cross-Examination

### Clash 1: Greenberg vs. Sperber & Wilson
**Do cross-linguistic universals establish a comprehension floor, or does relevance-guided inference recover missing elements?**

**Greenberg:** Universal 38 — that all languages mark negation by a dedicated word or morpheme — represents 1,151 independent language communities, none of which has converged on purely contextual negation recovery. This cannot be explained as coincidence or as cultural transmission from a common ancestor: the languages in the WALS sample represent all six major language families and dozens of isolates. The convergence on overt negation marking implies a processing constraint: the human comprehension system requires an overt anchor for negation in order to reliably process negative propositions. If relevance-guided inference could reliably recover negation from context alone, we would expect to find at least some language communities that exploit this — they do not exist.

**Sperber & Wilson:** The universals describe attested production conventions in natural languages under full acquisition conditions across entire speech communities. They do not constitute a controlled experiment on whether negation can be recovered inferentially by a cooperative, attentive hearer. Universal 38 establishes that no natural language has adopted a community-wide convention of implicit negation. It does not establish that an individual hearer, in a cooperative communicative context with high shared knowledge, cannot recover a negative proposition from context. The situations are not equivalent. Relevance Theory's claim is not that negation should be dropped in all contexts — it is that the decision about whether to retain or drop any element depends on the cognitive effect/effort ratio in the specific context, not on cross-linguistic production statistics.

Moreover, the experimental pragmatics evidence bears on this directly. Noveck & Sperber (2004) demonstrated that the processing of pragmatic inferences is context-sensitive — hearers who share more context with the speaker perform faster and more accurately on pragmatic inference tasks. In a written, cooperative, task-response context with high shared knowledge, the inferential recovery of implicit negation from a question like "Is evolution true?" followed by "Darwin wrong" is achievable at lower cost than in a natural conversation between strangers.

**Greenberg's reply:** I accept the methodological distinction. The universals address production conventions, not controlled comprehension experiments. My strongest surviving claim is not about negation marking per se but about word order: Universal 1, combined with Gibson's (2000) Dependency Locality Theory, provides psycholinguistic evidence — not just cross-linguistic production statistics — that canonical word order minimises integration costs during online parsing. This is a direct processing claim, not merely a distributional one. Deviation from SVO imposes syntactic integration costs that are not reduced by relevance-guided inference because they occur at the sub-propositional level of parsing, before inferential processes engage.

**Resolution of Clash 1:** Greenberg's strongest surviving claim concerns word order, not negation. SVO order is supported by both cross-linguistic convergence and psycholinguistic processing evidence. The negation-marking claim reduces from an absolute comprehension floor to a probabilistic recommendation supported by distributional evidence. Relevance Theory's position — that any element can in principle be recovered inferentially in a cooperative context — is not refuted by the universals, but the universals provide strong probabilistic evidence that some elements are reliably more expensive to recover than others.

### Clash 2: Halliday vs. Labov
**Halliday says the interpersonal metafunction can be zeroed; Labov says the vernacular is most systematic but retains evaluation structure. Who is right about what can be stripped?**

**Halliday:** My claim is categorical: interpersonal metafunction tokens — modal verbs, stance adjuncts, epistemic hedges, attribution phrases, social acknowledgements — carry no ideational content in task-response contexts and can be zeroed without propositional loss. This is not a claim about all communicative contexts: in a medical consultation, "you might want to consider" performs essential interpersonal work by managing power asymmetry and patient autonomy. In a task-response AI context, the same phrase is purely stylistic overhead. The metafunctions are context-sensitive in their relative weight; in this context, the interpersonal function is vestigial.

**Labov:** I partially agree, but the category of Evaluation in Labov & Waletzky (1967) is not reducible to interpersonal metafunction in Halliday's sense. Evaluation in the narrative structure framework performs two distinct functions. The interpersonal function — "this is important because" — is indeed droppable overhead. But Evaluation also performs a **textual** function: it flags to the hearer that the Complicating Action they have just processed should be understood as relevant to their concerns, not merely as a series of events. This flagging is related to what Sperber & Wilson call the communicative presumption of relevance — it is a signal that the cognitive effects of the preceding content are worth the processing effort. In contexts where the query itself establishes relevance clearly — "Explain photosynthesis" establishes that photosynthesis is the relevant topic — Evaluation is redundant and droppable. In contexts where the relevance of the answer to the user's deeper goal is non-obvious, some minimal evaluation is necessary.

**Halliday's reply:** Labov's distinction is correct and it maps onto an SFG distinction that I should have made explicit. When Evaluation is performing textual cohesion work — flagging relevance, connecting the Complicating Action to the hearer's broader concerns — it is a textual metafunction token, not an interpersonal one. Textual cohesion devices are partially necessary (as I argued in my opening statement). The operative question is which function any given evaluative sentence is performing. In most direct task queries, the evaluation is interpersonal stance ("this is important because") rather than textual cohesion, and it is droppable. My instruction stands: zero the interpersonal tier; the textual tier requires case-by-case assessment.

**Resolution of Clash 2:** Both agents agree that interpersonal tokens are dispensable. The residual disagreement concerns evaluative framing. Halliday's mapping of Evaluation onto the interpersonal metafunction was incomplete: Evaluation can be textual. The operative distinction is whether the significance of the answer is established by the query context (making Evaluation redundant) or whether it requires explicit flagging (making a minimal evaluative sentence necessary). For direct task queries where the user's goal is transparent from the question, Evaluation is redundant and droppable.

### Clash 3: Sperber & Wilson vs. Halliday
**RT says any element can be dropped if relevance is maintained; Halliday says textual cohesion devices are partially necessary. Where is the line?**

**Sperber & Wilson:** The cognitive effect/effort calculation applies to cohesive devices just as to any other token. "Furthermore" should be dropped when the additive relation between two propositions is already inferrable from their content — when the hearer's relevance-guided processing will connect them without the connective as an explicit instruction. The criterion is not whether the connective appears in Halliday's cohesive system or whether it contributes to cross-sentence reference chains. The criterion is marginal cognitive effect: does this token shift the set of contextual implications derivable from the utterance? If not, it is eliminable.

The Bott & Noveck (2004) evidence establishes that pragmatic inference carries a processing cost. But the cost of inferring an additive or causal relation between two topically adjacent propositions is far lower than the cost of computing a scalar implicature — the type of inference measured in that study. Recovering that "Plant absorb CO2" and "Plant use CO2 for glucose synthesis" stand in a causal relation requires one inferential step from world knowledge. Recovering "some but not all" from "some" requires a sophisticated pragmatic move (Q-implicature calculation) that engages the quantity scale. The two operations are not computationally equivalent, and the Bott & Noveck data should not be generalised to all forms of pragmatic inference.

**Halliday:** The Hasan (1984) data is directly relevant here. Hasan analysed cohesive harmony in naturally occurring texts rated for coherence by naive readers. Texts with lower cohesive density — fewer conjunctive devices, fewer reference chains, less lexical reiteration — were rated as less coherent even when their propositional content was equivalent. This establishes an empirical threshold: below a certain cohesive density, perceived coherence drops. The threshold is not identified precisely by Hasan's data, but it is real and it is not zero.

The specific theoretical point: **semantic contrast** cannot be recovered from propositional content alone. "X is true. However, Y is also true" — the word "however" signals that the speaker considers Y unexpected given X, or that X and Y are in tension. This cannot be inferred from the propositional content of X and Y taken independently, because any two propositions are compatible with a contrast relation or not depending on the speaker's epistemic state. The contrast must be lexically marked. Additive conjunction ("furthermore") is different: two propositions that share a topic and are both asserted by the same speaker are interpreted as additive by default. The "furthermore" adds near-zero cognitive effect. But "however" adds genuine cognitive effect — it signals a non-obvious inferential relation — and should be retained.

**Sperber & Wilson's reply:** The semantic contrast point is well taken. We accept that "however" carries cognitive effect that cannot be recovered from propositional content alone, because it encodes the speaker's assessment of the expectedness of the second proposition given the first — and this is a genuinely new propositional element that the hearer cannot recover from world knowledge alone. This is a case where the connective's removal would force costly inferential work that fails. We maintain that additive connectives ("furthermore", "additionally") and sequential connectives ("then", "next") are recoverable from propositional sequence and topic coherence, and their removal imposes only minimal processing overhead.

**Resolution of Clash 3:** The line falls at semantic contrast. Contrast markers ("however", "but", "yet", "nevertheless") carry cognitive effect not recoverable from adjacent propositional content and should be retained. Additive markers ("furthermore", "additionally", "also") and sequential markers ("then", "next", "subsequently") are recoverable from propositional structure when adjacent clauses share a topic, and can be replaced by visual layout (bullet adjacency, line breaks).

## Part 3 — Revised Positions

**Sperber & Wilson (revised):** Core claim stands: drop tokens in order of marginal cognitive effect. Cross-examination with Greenberg establishes one limitation: canonical SVO word order should be retained not merely as a pragmatic choice but as a response to sub-propositional syntactic integration costs (Gibson 2000) that relevance-guided inference does not address — these occur before inferential processes engage. Cross-examination with Halliday establishes a second constraint: semantic contrast markers should be retained because they carry cognitive effect (the speaker's assessment of unexpectedness) that is genuinely non-recoverable from propositional content alone.

**Halliday (revised):** Interpersonal tier: zero completely in task-response context. Textual tier: retain semantic contrast markers ("however", "but") that cannot be realised by visual layout; replace additive and sequential markers with bullet structure and line breaks. Ideational tier: retain fully. Concession to Labov: Evaluation is droppable when the query establishes relevance clearly; it may be necessary in its minimal textual cohesion form when the relevance of the answer to the user's deeper goal is non-obvious.

**Greenberg (revised):** Strongest surviving claim shifts from negation marking to word order: SVO is supported by both cross-linguistic convergence and psycholinguistic processing evidence (Gibson 2000) and should be retained. Article dropping and inflection dropping remain fully supported — both are absent in the majority of WALS languages. Overt negation marking is retained as a strong recommendation (supported by the full WALS survey) rather than an absolute comprehension floor.

**Labov (revised):** Style Axiom prediction survives: AI output is maximal high-attention style and is therefore systematically the least efficient form of English available. The Labovian minimum — Complicating Action + Resolution — survives cross-examination as the obligatory core of AI response structure. Concession to Halliday: when Evaluation is performing textual cohesion work (signalling non-obvious relevance), a minimal evaluative clause is permitted, but this case is rare in direct task queries.

## Part 4 — Derived System Prompt Instructions

**Sperber & Wilson:** Drop connective, evaluative, and meta-commentary tokens first; these carry near-zero marginal cognitive effect when propositional relations are inferrable from adjacent clause content (Carston 2002), whereas propositional tokens carry cognitive effects that cannot be inferred from context and whose omission forces processing costs exceeding the relevance threshold.

**Halliday:** Zero all interpersonal metafunction tokens — modal hedges, stance adjuncts, epistemic attribution phrases, and social acknowledgements — as they carry no ideational content in task-response contexts (Halliday & Matthiessen 2004); replace additive and sequential textual tokens with bullet layout and line breaks; retain only semantic contrast markers from the textual tier.

**Greenberg:** Use canonical SVO word order in every clause; mark negation overtly with a lexical item; omit articles, inflectional agreement suffixes, and auxiliary verbs used only for tense agreement, as these categories are absent in the majority of the world's 2,662 documented languages (WALS, Dryer & Haspelmath 2013) without comprehension impairment.

**Labov:** Produce only Complicating Action and Resolution; omit Abstract (do not restate the query), Orientation (do not provide unsolicited background), Evaluation (do not frame significance), and Coda (do not add closing remarks) — the four optional components of Labov & Waletzky's (1967) narrative structure constitute systematic high-attention stylistic overhead with no propositional content.

## Part 5 — Round 1 Synthesis

**What survived cross-examination:**

1. SVO order is non-negotiable — confirmed by both Greenberg's cross-linguistic universals (Universal 1; WALS) and Sperber & Wilson's acceptance that syntactic integration costs (Gibson 2000 DLT) are sub-propositional and not recovered by relevance-guided inference.
2. Propositional tokens must not be dropped — confirmed by Bott & Noveck (2004): inferential recovery of propositional content is costly in ways that connective recovery is not.
3. Interpersonal tier can be zeroed — confirmed by Halliday (no ideational content) and corroborated by Labov (high-attention stylistic overhead with no propositional content).
4. Semantic contrast markers must be retained — confirmed in the Sperber & Wilson vs. Halliday clash: "however" carries cognitive effect (speaker's assessment of unexpectedness) not recoverable from propositional content.
5. Additive and sequential connectives can be replaced by visual layout — confirmed; their propositional relations are inferrable from topic coherence and adjacency.
6. Complicating Action + Resolution is the obligatory narrative core — Labov's claim survives; Abstract, Orientation, Coda are pure stylistic overhead; Evaluation is droppable in direct task queries.
7. Articles and inflectional morphology are dispensable — Greenberg's WALS evidence uncontested in cross-examination.

**What was eliminated or reduced:**

- Greenberg's absolute comprehension floor on overt negation marking reduced from a categorical rule to a strong recommendation (probabilistic, not absolute).
- Halliday's claim that all textual cohesion devices can be replaced by visual layout does not survive — semantic contrast ("however") must be lexically marked.
